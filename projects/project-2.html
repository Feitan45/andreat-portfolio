Project Overview

Problem

Envisioned to be a comprehensive online database, Criptionary aims to be a centralized space to document and spotlight disability concepts, distinguished visionaries, and pioneering advocates. More than a passive dictionary, it is a living wiki, constantly evolving by a community anchored in disability advocacy.

Research

Methods

  • Google Survey Form (n=24 people)
  • Interviews with disability community members from UC Berkeley Lab (n=10)

Pain Points

Needs

Key user quote

“I get conflicting or vague information, and no one clearly explains what I actually need to do to get a diagnosis or access resources.”

Ideation

Themes

  • easy navigation and searchability



  • want accurate, specific information

  • accessibility-friendly website layout and navigation

Design Principles

  • Navigation Bar – Menu option with key sections, such as About, Glossary Term section, section for inspiring News/Stories of advocates with disability, Contact
  • Profile Login - community-based and collaborative editing for up-to-date, accurate information
  • Responsive design, contrast color palettes, scalable dyslexic-friendly fonts, and more.

Exploration (Iterations)

Issues

  • Main Page is too cluttered,  contributing to cognitive overload, overwhelming users and reducing focus. 
  • Too much features in one scan results in complex navigation, as screen readers process content sequentially.
  • Having too much text, links, and content in one page results in difficult  screen reader and keyboard navigation. 

Decision Rationale

  • Less Content – display essential information, and the rest is hidden away. 
  • Sequential / Scrolling Items – reduce cognitive load, easier navigation, greater accessibility for screen readers. 
  • Reduced Sections to only Glossary to improve MVP mission and introduction of too many features. 

Final Screens

Design Elements

Colors

  • explored color palettes for calm, universal color
  • tested color-blind friendliness by checking with Adobe Color-Blind Feature and converted colors into grayscale to analyze the contrast.
  • decided Blue/Yellow/Orange color palette for better diversity in color. 

Typeface

  • explored dyslexic-friendly fonts, including Open-Dyslexic, a typeface specifically designed to reduce dyslexic symptoms.
  • chose a round-type typeface; Arvo for a bold heading and Poppins semibold for paragraph, subheader.

Reflection

What went well

  • Designed for accessibility and usability
  • Explored many common accessibility issues and solutions
  • Simplified designs for fast engineering implementation

What I’d improve next

  • Expand collaborative editing + personalization
  • Support editing review and version history for accuracy and accountability
  • Greater accessibility features and considerations
